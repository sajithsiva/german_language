Final devoicing (Auslautverhärtung)

Meaning

Auslautverhärtung - Final devoicing

Pronunciation

ഔസ്‌ലൗട്ട്‌-ഫെർ-ഹാ-ടുങ്

Examples

  • Written: Pfand
  • Spoken: /pfant/ with t, not d.
  • Because the d is at the end, German turns it into a voiceless sound (t).
  • Compare:
  • Pfand → /pfant/ (final “d” becomes “t”)
  • Pfänder → /pfɛndɐ/ (here the “d” is in the middle → stays d)
  • Rad (wheel) → pronounced /raːt/
  • Räder (wheels) → pronounced /rɛːdɐ/ (the d stays voiced)
  • More examples:
  • Hund (dog) - /hʊnt/ - d → t at word end
  • Kind (child) - /kɪnt/ - d → t
  • Hand (hand) - /hant/ - d → t
  • Hand (hand) - /hant/ - d → t
  • Tag (day) - /tak/ - g → k
  • ab (off) - /ap/ - b → p

Notes

Final devoicing (Auslautverhärtung)

In German, voiced consonants (like b, d, g) become voiceless (p, t, k) when they appear at the end of a word.

A voiceless sound is a sound you make without vibrating your vocal cords.

Voiceless sounds are made with no vibration in the throat — only air.

Voiced consonant Becomes → Voiceless consonant Example
b → p ab → /ap/
d → t Hund → /hʊnt/
g → k Tag → /tak/
v (spoken like /v/) → f nerv → /nɛrf/
s (voiced /z/) → s (voiceless) Hase ends with /s/ sound
sch (ʒ) (rare) → sch (ʃ) in some foreign words
